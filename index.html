FAQ ›
Quick safari FAQ reference. Open a section, then tap a question to view the answer.
General Information ›
Do I need a visa to enter a new country?

Visa requirements vary by destination and passport. We recommend arranging visas before travel whenever possible. Keep your passport, visa confirmation, and entry documents accessible while traveling.

What should I do if my flight is delayed?

Contact us as soon as possible if your arrival changes. When we know about a delay, we can adjust your airport greeting, transfers, and onward arrangements.

Will a guide or representative meet me at the airport?

Yes. A Prominent Africa representative will meet you after you clear customs and collect your luggage. Please keep us updated if your flight details change.

What should I do if my luggage arrives late or is lost?

Complete the airline’s delayed or lost luggage report before leaving the airport. Share the claim information with us, and we will help coordinate delivery once your luggage arrives.

Can the daily schedule change?

Yes. Steve may adjust timing and routing for wildlife, weather, road conditions, or flight updates while protecting the overall journey.

Money Matters ›
Which currency is accepted?

Tanzanian shillings are useful for small local purchases. U.S. dollars are widely accepted at hotels, safari lodges, camps, and many tourism businesses.

Should I carry cash?

Yes. Carry a sensible amount of cash in smaller denominations for gratuities, small purchases, and locations where card terminals or internet service may be unavailable.

Will credit cards work?

Major cards are generally accepted at larger hotels, lodges, and resorts. Processing fees may apply, and service can be interrupted by weak connectivity.

Are older U.S. dollar notes accepted?

Bring clean, undamaged U.S. dollar notes issued in 2009 or later. Older, marked, or torn notes may be refused.

Safety & Health Matters ›
What should I expect on game drives?

Roads can be dusty, uneven, and bumpy. Use the vehicle handholds, follow your guide’s instructions, and tell Steve whenever you need a comfort stop.

How should I manage prescription medication?

Carry enough medication for the full journey, keep it in original packaging, and pack it in your carry-on luggage.

Can I drink the tap water?

Use sealed bottled or properly filtered water for drinking and brushing your teeth unless your accommodation confirms that its tap water is safe.

Do I need travel insurance?

We strongly recommend comprehensive travel insurance that includes medical treatment, emergency evacuation, delays, cancellations, and unexpected trip disruption.

💵 Gratuities & Tipping ›

Gratuities are an important part of the hospitality and tourism industry throughout East Africa and are a meaningful way to recognize exceptional service. While tipping is always at your discretion, it is customary and greatly appreciated by the guides, lodge staff, and hospitality teams who help make your journey memorable.

The recommendations below reflect customary practices throughout East Africa and are intended as a helpful guide rather than an expectation.

East Africa Safari ›
  • Private Safari Guide: US$10–25 per person, per day.
  • Lodge or Camp Staff: US$20 per person, per day through the shared staff tip box.
  • Hotel Staff: US$5 per person.
  • Private Airport or Hotel Transfer: US$5 per vehicle.
  • Most safari lodges and camps provide a shared tip box near reception so the full hospitality team is recognized.
  • Your private safari guide is usually tipped directly at the conclusion of the safari portion of your journey.
Additional Tipping Situations ›
  • Airport porter or baggage handler: Around US$2 per bag when assistance is provided.
  • Hotel bell staff: Around US$2 per bag.
  • Hotel housekeeping: US$1–2 per room, per day; leaving it daily is helpful because staff may change.
  • Restaurant server: 5–10% when a service charge is not already included.
  • Taxi driver: Round up the fare or add US$2–5 for exceptional help, especially with luggage.
  • Spa or local activity staff: US$2–5 for good service, at your discretion.
Additional Guidance ›
  • Bring newer U.S. dollar bills issued in 2009 or later.
  • Carry a variety of small denominations, especially US$1, US$5, US$10, and US$20 notes.
  • Use clean, undamaged notes; torn, heavily marked, or older bills may be refused.
  • Cash is usually the simplest way to tip safari guides, transfer drivers, porters, and lodge teams.
  • You may tip throughout the journey or at the conclusion of each stay, according to what feels most comfortable.
  • Gratuities should always reflect your personal experience and the service received.
  • Local currency is appropriate for restaurants, taxis, and small gratuities.
  • Check restaurant and hotel bills for included service charges.
  • Guides are usually tipped directly, while lodge and camp teams generally share a tip box.
  • “Asante” means “thank you” in Swahili.
  • When uncertain, please ask us.

A Final Note

Above all, gratuities should reflect your personal experience. While these recommendations represent customary practices throughout East Africa, exceptional service may inspire additional recognition, and every gratuity is a meaningful expression of appreciation for the people who work hard to make your journey unforgettable.
